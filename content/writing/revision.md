## Sentence

### Find the verb

1. Find the verb in the sentence.
2. Put “who” or “what” in front of the verb to identify who performs the action. If the answer is not a main character—a person, system, or concrete thing—revise.
3. Make sure the verb names a real action, not a vague placeholder. Verbs like *is*, *has*, *occurs*, and *exists* often signal that the real action is buried in a nearby noun.

Nominalizations are nouns formed from verbs: *investigation* (investigate), *development* (develop), *decision* (decide). When you spot one, convert it back to a verb and rewrite the sentence around it.

#### Tips

Use these strategies when you find a nominalization or a weak verb:

- [Subject of an empty verb](../actions/#subject-of-empty-verb) — the nominalization is the subject of *is*, *has*, or *seems*
- [Follows empty verb](../actions/#follows-empty-verb) — the nominalization comes after a vague verb like *conducted* or *made*
- [Subject of empty verb followed by nominalization](../actions/#subject-of-empty-verb-followed-by-nominalization) — both the subject and object are nominalizations
- [There is/there are](../actions/#there-isthere-are) — the sentence opens with an expletive construction
- [Nominalizations joined by prepositions](../actions/#nominalizations-joined-by-prepositions) — two nominalizations connected by *of*

Not all nominalizations are problems. Refer to [Useful Nominalizations](../actions#useful-nominalizations) for cases where keeping them is the right call.

### Active vs Passive

Active voice is the default: the subject performs the action. Use passive voice only when one of these situations applies:

- **The doer doesn't matter.** When the actor is unknown, unimportant, or obvious, passive lets you foreground the result. See [Does the doer matter?](../active-passive#does-the-doer-matter)
- **Passive helps with sentence transitions.** Start a sentence with a word introduced at the end of the previous sentence. Passive voice lets you move that familiar word into the subject position. See [Sentence transitions](../active-passive#sentence-transitions).
- **Passive maintains a consistent character.** If you are telling a story from one character's perspective, passive keeps that character in the subject across sentences. See [Consistent character](../active-passive#consistent-character).

### Cohesion

Cohesion is the sense that sentences flow into one another. It comes from a single structural habit: end each sentence with information that sets up the beginning of the next. When readers reach the start of a new sentence, they should recognize something from where they just were.

{{< admonition "Cohesion > Clarity" tip >}}
There are three principles of clarity:
1. Make main characters the subjects of sentences.
2. Make important actions verbs.
3. Put old information before new information.

If you have to choose among these principles, choose the third. The cohesion of a passage is always more important than the clarity of individual sentences.
{{< /admonition >}}

- **Begin with familiar information.** Familiar information is what the reader already knows: either from the sentence they just read or from general knowledge they bring to the text. See [Old and new information](../cohesion-coherence#old-and-new-information).
- **End with new information.** Save what the reader cannot yet predict for the end of the sentence, where it receives the most attention.
- **Simple to complex.** Familiar information tends to be simple; new information tends to be complex. Ordering a sentence this way eases readers into difficult material.


### Coherence

Coherence describes how all the sentences in a passage add up to a unified picture. Where cohesion governs how one sentence connects to the next, coherence governs whether the passage as a whole stays focused on a consistent set of characters and ideas.

[Topic](../cohesion-coherence#topics) is a psychological term: it refers to the idea that readers expect each sentence to be about something specific. State the topic near the beginning of the sentence to make the subject clear.

The subject of each sentence should contain the main characters. When subjects drift from sentence to sentence, readers lose the thread even if each individual sentence is grammatically correct.

#### Finding characters and topics

Before you write, list the characters and concepts you plan to write about. These are the subjects that should appear at the beginning of most sentences in the passage.

To identify your topics, give the passage a title. The words in the title often name the most important characters and concepts.


#### Tips

Use these methods to make sentences and paragraphs coherent:

- **Use transitional words** to signal logical relationships between sentences: _next_, _because_, _however_, _so_, _therefore_, _also_, and _finally_. These words tell readers how two ideas connect, so readers do not have to infer the relationship themselves.
- **Focus each paragraph on one idea.** If a paragraph covers two ideas, split it.
- **Subordinate less-important details.** Use _which_, _that_, and _when_ to attach secondary information to the main clause rather than giving it its own sentence.

### Emphasis

Readers naturally stress the last few words of a sentence. That stress position is where meaning lands hardest. If weak or redundant words occupy the end of the sentence, the reader emphasizes the wrong thing. Get to the subject and verb quickly so readers can carry complex information through to the end:

```
(optional intro phrase) > short, concrete subject > action verb > emphasized complex information
```

#### Revision strategies

Because the end of the sentence carries the most weight, revise it carefully:

- **Trim the end.** Remove unnecessary words that dilute the stress position.
- **Shift peripheral ideas left.** Move weak qualifiers or afterthoughts toward the beginning of the sentence.
- **Shift new information right.** New information belongs at the end, where readers pay the most attention.

#### Devices

Use these devices to move the right words into the stress position:

- **Key concepts first.** Put the [key concepts](../emphasis/#topics-stress-themes-and-coherence) of the passage in the stress position of the opening sentence to signal what the whole passage is about.
- **There shift.** Begin with "There is/are" to push new or complex information toward the end. See [There shift](../emphasis#there-shift).
- **Passive voice.** Flip subject and object to move new information later in the sentence. See [Passives](../emphasis#passives).
- **What shift.** Use "What [subject] [verb] is..." to shift emphasis to the end. See [What shift](../emphasis#what-shift).
- **It shift.** Move a long noun clause to the end and open with *It*. See [It shift](../emphasis#it-shift).
- **Not only X, but Y.** Use this construction to emphasize the second element of a pair. See [Not only X, but Y as well](../emphasis#not-only-x-but-y-as-well).
- **Pronoun substitution.** Replace a repeated word with a pronoun so the stress position stays fresh. See [Pronoun substitution](../emphasis#pronoun-substitution).


### Concision

Concise writing uses only as many words as the idea requires. Apply these rules when editing:

- **Delete meaningless words.** Cut filler words that add length without adding meaning: *kind of*, *basically*, *really*, *various*, *given*. See [Delete meaningless words](../concision#delete-meaningless-words).
- **Delete doubled words.** Writers sometimes pair synonyms for emphasis. Pick one: *hope and desire* becomes *hope*; *each and every* becomes *each*. See [Delete doubled words](../concision#delete-doubled-words).
- **Delete what readers can infer.** Cut redundant modifiers (*future plans*, *free gift*) and redundant categories (*blue in color*, *period of time*). See [Delete what readers can infer](../concision#delete-what-readers-can-infer).
- **Replace a phrase with a word.** Wordy phrases often have a single-word equivalent: *the reason for* becomes *why*. See [Replace a phrase with a word](../concision#replace-a-phrase-with-a-word).
- **Replace negatives with affirmatives.** Negative constructions require more mental work. *Not the same* becomes *different*; *not often* becomes *rarely*. See [Replace negatives with affirmatives](../concision#replace-negatives-with-affirmatives).
- **Delete adjectives and adverbs.** Cut every adjective and adverb, then restore only those the reader needs. See [Delete adjectives and adverbs](../concision#delete-adjectives-and-adverbs).

Watch your qualifiers:

- **Hedges** soften claims (*usually*, *might*, *appears*). A few are appropriate; too many make you sound uncertain. See [Hedges](../concision#hedges).
- **Intensifiers** strengthen claims (*very*, *clearly*, *obviously*). Overusing them weakens your writing. See [Intensifiers](../concision#intensifiers).

### Shape

A well-shaped sentence opens with a short, direct subject and verb, then extends into complex or new information. Readers lose their footing when the main clause arrives too late, gets interrupted, or drags on after the point is made.

Follow these rules:

- **Place the point near the beginning.** Open with a short, concrete subject followed directly by a verb. See [Starting with your point](../shape#starting-with-your-point).
- **Get to the verb quickly.** Long openings, including subordinate clauses, abstract subjects, and heavy noun phrases, make readers wait for the action. See [How to revise openings](../shape#how-to-revise-openings).
- **Do not tack on phrases and subordinate clauses after the verb.** Endings that pile on relative clauses weaken the sentence. Cut them, turn them into separate sentences, or use resumptive, summative, or free modifiers. See [How to revise endings](../shape#how-to-revise-endings).
- **Do not interrupt the subject-verb or verb-object connection.** Move interrupting phrases to the beginning or end of the sentence. See [Get to the verb and object](../shape#get-to-the-verb-and-object).

A dangling modifier occurs when the implied subject of an opening phrase does not match the subject of the main clause. Name the subject explicitly to fix it. See [Dangling modifiers](../shape#dangling-modifiers).
